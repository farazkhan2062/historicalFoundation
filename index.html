Pre-Pakistan Education System (712–1947)

Education in the subcontinent began with the arrival of Muslims in 712 AD. Maktabs and Madrasahs taught Qur’an, Arabic, Persian, mathematics, astronomy, and medicine. The Mughal era marked the golden age of education, while British rule introduced English-based modern education and universities.

Sir Syed Ahmed Khan – Educational Reformer

Sir Syed Ahmed Khan (1817–1898) was the pioneer of modern Muslim education. He founded the Muhammadan Anglo-Oriental College in 1875, later Aligarh Muslim University. He encouraged scientific thinking, English education, and social reform among Muslims.

Education After Independence (1947–Present)

After independence, Pakistan faced challenges such as illiteracy and lack of institutions. Major reforms include the 1947 Education Conference, 1959 Commission, establishment of HEC, and Article 25-A under the 18th Amendment ensuring free education for ages 5–16.

Education During Bhutto Era (1972–1977)

During Zulfikar Ali Bhutto’s government, education was nationalized and declared free. Enrollment increased, but challenges such as lack of resources, political interference, weak administration, and decline in quality also emerged.
